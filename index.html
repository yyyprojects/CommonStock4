Common vs. Preferred Stock

Companies issue two main types of stock. While both represent ownership, they offer very different deals to investors. The biggest difference? Who gets paid first if the company goes under.

The Great Trade-Off: Risk vs. Reward

Common stock is famous for its potential growth, but that comes with volatility. The stock market can be a rollercoaster in the short term, but historically, it has rewarded long-term investors.

This illustrates how an investment can experience sharp ups and downs (volatility) day-to-day, but can trend upwards over many years (capital appreciation). The key is a long-term perspective.

Why Do Companies Issue Stock?

It's all about raising money to grow without taking on debt. By selling small pieces of ownership, companies can fund new projects, hire more people, and expand their business.

A company's funding is a mix of debt (loans) and equity (stock). Issuing stock increases the equity portion, strengthening the company's financial foundation.

Your Path to Becoming an Investor

Getting started is easier than you think. It's a journey that involves planning, learning, and taking measured steps. Here's a simplified roadmap.

1

Define Your Goals

What are you saving for? Retirement? A house? Knowing your goal and timeline is the most important first step.

2

Open a Brokerage Account

This is your gateway to the stock market. Many online brokers offer commission-free trading and have no minimum balance.

3

Fund Your Account

Start with an amount you're comfortable with. You can invest with as little as $100. Consistency is more important than a large starting sum.

4

Pick Your Investments

For beginners, a great starting point is a diversified ETF that tracks a major index like the S&P 500. This gives you "instant diversification" and reduces risk.
